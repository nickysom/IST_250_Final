Core Camera Settings

New to using a camera? Here's a breakdown of the core settings and concepts that will help you take better photos with confidence.

ISO Settings:

ISO controls your camera's sensitivity to light. A lower ISO (100–200) is great for bright settings, while a higher ISO (800+) is used in darker environments and scenes. A higher ISO can cause grainy or “noisy” images, which can make your photos off, if not used right.

Shutter Speed:

This controls how long your camera's sensor is exposed to light. Fast shutter speeds (like 1/1000s) freeze action, while slower ones (like 1/30s) blur motion and work better in low light. Faster shutter is good for capturing fast moving objects, like basketball games or cars. Slowler speeds can used for slow motion effects, like capturing a waterfall or stars.

Aperture Controls:

Aperture controls how wide the lens opens. A low f-stop (like f/1.8) gives you a blurry background and more light. A higher f-stop (like f/8 or f/11) keeps more of the image in focus and lets in less light. Lower aperture is good for portrait photos, where you want the background blurred. High aperture is good for shooting wider photos like landscapes or group photos.

Lighting Settings:

Lighting makes or breaks a photo. Natural light is soft and flattering. Indoors, use window light or diffused lamps. Avoid harsh shadows or overhead light unless that's the effect you want. You can also manually configure the light settings to help match your needs.

White Balance:

This setting affects the color tone of your photos. Choose “Daylight,” “Shade,” or “Tungsten” to match your environment, or use Auto White Balance (AWB) if you're unsure. Playing around with setting lighting can help you understand your camera more!

Focus Settings:

Most cameras have auto focus, but learning manual focus is helpful for tricky shots. Use single point focus for portraits and continuous focus for moving subjects. Autofocus is great for bright outdoor settings. Manual is great for when your lens is struggling to focus on a object. Things like close up photos, stars at night, or product photos are good examples of when to use this setting.

Understanding Camera Lenses

From left to right: 75-300mm Telephoto Lens, 18–55mm Kit Lens, 50mm Prime Lens

The Basic Lenses:

Lenses come in different focal lengths, and each one creates a unique look.

  • 18–55mm (Kit Lens): Versatile and great for general use. Good for landscapes, portraits, and everyday shots. Comes with most camera kits.
  • 50mm (Prime Lens): A favorite for portraits. It creates a natural perspective with beautiful background blur.
  • 70–200mm (Telephoto): Perfect for sports, wildlife, or capturing distant subjects. Great for background compression and soft backgrounds.
  • Macro Lenses: Used for close ups likeflowers, textures, insects, and product details.

Essential Accessories

Once you're comfortable with your camera, a few key accessories can make your photography easier, more creative, and more reliable. Here are some great ones to think about:

  • Tripod: Helps with low light photography, long exposures, self portraits, and stability for videos.
  • Camera Bag: Keeps your gear organized and protected. Look for one with padding and space for your camera, lenses, and accessories.
  • Extra Batteries: Always carry at least one fully charged spare. Cold weather and long shoots drain batteries quickly. Don't cheap out on batteries, the cheaper ones are known to explode...
  • Memory Cards: Use fast, high storage SD cards. Always keep backups ready. It's easier to have more storage than less.
  • Lens Filters: UV filters protect your glass. ND filters help with bright scenes. Polarizers reduce reflections and deepen skies. These are optional but very nice to have.
  • Cleaning Kit: A microfiber cloth, air blower, and lens wipes keep your gear clean and sharp.
